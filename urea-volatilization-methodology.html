How the urea volatilization calculator works

Jun 2026 · 2 min read

A decision aid that estimates how much broadcast urea nitrogen will be lost to ammonia volatilization over the next 14 days, and whether stabilizing with NBPT (e.g. Agrotain) is worth the cost.

Inputs

You provide (about the field):

  • Application rate (lb N/ac)
  • Soil pH
  • Soil texture (sand to clay)
  • Tillage system (no-till / minimum-till / conventional)
  • Nitrogen price ($/lb) and NBPT treatment cost ($/ac)

Pulled automatically (14-day local forecast, via Open-Meteo):

  • Soil temperature
  • Relative humidity (used as a surface-wetness proxy)
  • Precipitation

Key assumptions

  • Calibrated to Montana State University dryland field trials (Engel et al.; MSU Extension). Reference points it reproduces: ~18% average loss (range 1–44%), ~3% on a dry surface, ~40% under hot/humid conditions, and a ~45% realistic ceiling.
  • Rain incorporates urea: a single ≥0.5 in event moves N into the soil and effectively stops further loss; ~0.3 in partially helps.
  • Temperature drives the rate: urease activity roughly doubles per 10 °C.
  • Loss rises with higher soil pH, coarser / low-CEC soils, and no-till surface residue.
  • Humidity stands in for surface wetness because it’s forecast reliably across the full 14 days, unlike soil moisture.
  • NBPT delays, it doesn’t erase: it suppresses loss for a temperature-dependent lag (~7–14 days, shorter when warm). If no rain arrives within that window, losses resume — so NBPT’s value shrinks in prolonged dry spells.
  • Forecast confidence fades after ~7 days (especially for rain), so days 8–14 are flagged lower-confidence.

How it calculates the outcome

  1. Daily loss accrues from a “susceptible” pool of the applied N. Each day’s loss rate is a base rate multiplied by factors for temperature, humidity, pH, texture, and tillage (capped at a realistic daily maximum).
  2. It accumulates day by day until a rain event incorporates the urea (loss stops) or the 14-day window ends.
  3. Three scenarios are compared: apply untreated urea now; apply NBPT-stabilized urea now; wait and apply just before the next forecast rain.
  4. Two horizons are reported: a 5-day high-confidence figure and the full 14-day episode. The NBPT pay-off verdict is judged on the 14-day window, where the comparison is honest.
  5. Money: loss % × rate = lb N lost; × N price = dollars lost. NBPT’s net cost adds its treatment price. The recommendation is the lowest net cost.
This is an estimate, not a guarantee — field volatilization is highly variable, so every figure is shown as a range. It is calibrated to Montana dryland data and is not a substitute for a soil test or local agronomist.
